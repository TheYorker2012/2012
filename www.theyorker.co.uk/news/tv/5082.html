Doctor Who blog: The Pandorica Opens


Tuesday, 22nd June 2010
Lois Cameron
“There was a goblin, or a trickster, or a warrior. A nameless, terrible thing, soaked in the blood of a billion galaxies. The most feared being in all the cosmos. And nothing could stop it, or reason with it. One day it would just drop out of the sky and tear down your world.”

Well now, that really was something else entirely, wasn’t it? Some people have criticised this series of Doctor Who for the obviously cheap episodes but the production team were obviously saving their money for the beautiful, epic finale. And boy did it pay off.

Lots of fans hated Russell T. Davies’ finales, because they tended to be big epics, with a lot of heart but not a lot of sci-fi logic. Personally, I loved them, but then that’s because I’m actually rather soppy under my cynical exterior. Davies’ finales typified the best and the worst of his writing, and Moffat is definitely continuing that trend. The episode certainly had some great fun moments, such as the quick succession of cameos at the beginning (Vincent! Churchill! Liz Ten!) and River’s increasingly elaborate ways of getting in touch with the Doctor across the mists of time, although the best was definitely the Doctor’s reaction to Rory’s return.

But, while a welcome relief at times, the comedy wasn’t the thing we all wanted from a Moffat finale. The intricate plotting of his episodes up to this point left a lot of fans hoping for a brilliantly clever use of the series arc. And, unless they’re seriously picky, this would have had to satisfy them all. Threads from across the series were masterfully picked up, particularly the immensity of the darkness inside the Doctor from ‘Amy’s Choice’, and the fairytale tone that has pervaded it right from the moment the Doctor told Amelia Pond in the first episode that she had a brilliant, fairytale name. Watch the episode again, knowing that the Pandorica is destined for the Doctor, and the whole thing – all of the Doctor’s speeches about the Pandorica – makes a disturbingly large amount of sense.

Serious praise has to go the core cast. Arthur Darvill reminded me of exactly why I’d warmed to Rory, while Karen Gillan played Amy’s confused happiness impeccably. But really, it was Matt Smith’s moment, and he took it completely. The big speeches, the gentle nudging of Rory, his heartbreak and despair at realising the Alliance’s plan for the Pandorica – all were wonderfully played by Smith and his majestically old face.

The last few moments of this episode, played in slow motion backed by a gorgeous score, were some of the most brilliant and heart-wrenching that the show has produced – Amy dead at the hands of the Auton Rory, River stuck in the exploding TARDIS and the Doctor, the one continual beacon of hope in the series, dragged into the Pandorica and sealed in. I’m dying to find out what happens next, but I also really don’t want this mad and rather wonderful fairytale to end.
